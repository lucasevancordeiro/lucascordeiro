About

A little about me

I have always been curious about things that sometimes people overlook. Why Singapore has more animal species than people realise. What it feels like to eat a bug. How a bamboo shoot experiences the seasons. I find these questions hard to stop thinking about.

My favourite podcasts are Wow in the World and Boring History for Sleep. One made me realise that science is full of things that sound impossible but aren't. The other taught me that even boring things have a story worth telling, if you look hard enough. At some point, I started making my own. It's called Singapore Science Stories on Spotify. I research the topics myself, and find a way to explain them so that other people find them as interesting as I do.

Most of what I build or write comes from the same place. I notice something that's hard to understand, and I want to fix that. Not because it's a school project, but for my own interest.

Game · Built on Scratch · International Coding Showcase, Silver · 2025

Climate Crusaders

A game that teaches players how different environmental problems need different scientific solutions. You pick the right solution for each climate issue and learn how Singapore handles it in real life. I built it on Scratch. Original concept. It's a topic close to my heart, as a young person who will inherit this earth.

The plan

Map every climate issue I wanted to cover to a real Singapore response. Build the game loop first, then the levels, then the visuals. Original code, and I created the art.

What worked

The core mechanic. Picking solutions and learning the reason behind each one made the game feel like a quiz that taught you something instead of testing you. When I got my friends to play, they remembered the science afterwards. That was the point.

What failed

The animations broke first. Sprites jittered when characters moved between zones. Movement felt sluggish even when the logic was right.

How I fixed it

I stripped each animation back to the simplest version that still read clearly, then layered detail on top. I rebuilt the movement system so motion stayed consistent across screens. Many rounds of trial and error.

What I learnt

Hours of debugging are part of the work. I learnt that the first idea is rarely the right one. And patience is a skill.

Science podcast · Creator and host · Spotify · 2025–present

Singapore Science Stories

I'm a huge fan of podcasts so I created this to explore science in Singapore in a way that other kids might enjoy listening to. I work on it independently: research the topic, write the scripts, record and edit the episodes on GarageBand. My parents then help me upload it onto Spotify. The series explores topics such as coral reef conservation, disappearing fireflies, edible insects, and environmental technology.

What worked

Researching each episode taught me that good science communication is not just about sharing facts. The episodes became stronger when I focused on questions that connected science to everyday life in Singapore.

What failed

Sometimes the mic quality was inconsistent, with certain parts being softer or louder than others, and balancing background music with speaking audio was challenging.

How I fixed it

I asked my dad for help with GarageBand editing. I learnt how to improve audio quality by adjusting volume levels, and balancing music so that it supports the narration without overpowering it. This helped make the episodes more enjoyable to listen to.

Favourite moment

The episode with my grandmother as my guest. That was the first episode that wasn't scripted. We ate edible bugs together. She vehemently hated it, then admitted it was tasty. Her reaction made the episode funny and memorable, and it showed me how science discussions can become more engaging when shared with other people.

Stair-climbing bot

I'm building a robot that can carry things up the stairs at home. I got the idea off a chair that can carry people up the stairs. Notably, they are expensive and have to be drilled into walls. So, I wanted to make a smaller version that is more accessible and useful for daily housework tasks, like helping my grandparents carry items onto the second floor.

The plan

A bot that uses arms to lift itself up. Carries weight on its top. Battery-powered. Programmed in LEGO Mindstorms.

What works

The lifting motion and the motor control. The top can hold a small bag. On a low ledge, the climb sequence runs end to end.

Web app · AI-assisted build · Personal project · 2025–2026 · Built on Base44

The Little Newsroom

I found it hard to use real news sites when I needed to prepare for Oral exams. I wanted to find out what's going on in the world, but there was no specific section on The Straits Times' site catered to students, and not everything on their homepage was suitable. I designed a web app that pulls real news from five live news sources, filtered for kids my age. I designed the idea, picked the sources, decided what to filter out, then figured out how the page would work. I used Base44 to write the code.

The plan

News had to be real, from real sources and not made up. Articles had to be suitable for kids around twelve, no graphic crime, no adult finance. Singapore news as well as world news. Cool science news. Articles would link back to its source.

What worked

Five RSS feeds (BBC Newsround, BBC World, CNA, NASA, Science Daily) gave enough stories. rss2json.com fixed a browser problem called CORS that was blocking the feeds. A two-layer filter caught different inappropriate content: keywords dropped stories like COE news, an AI classifier removed things like distressing world events.

What failed

The first version looked like it worked, but actually AI had invented sample articles to make the site look full. After I deleted them, they kept showing because Base44 had hardcoded them into the homepage.

How I fixed it

I traced the fake articles to the database and the homepage code. I switched to rss2json.com for the CORS problem. Base44's free plan blocks scheduled fetching, so I built a manual "Fetch Now" button and a "Last refreshed" timestamp.

What I learnt about working with AI

Working with AI is its own kind of skill. You have to know what to ask, what to remove, and when to step back and decide for yourself. AI will make things up, which I learnt to verify across more than one AI tool. Catching the AI mistakes — invented articles, hardcoded samples, silent filtering — felt like engineering.

Diary of a Bamboo Shoot

Before I started writing, I read about different plants and worked out which one would carry the story I wanted to tell.

Everything in the story is scientifically accurate. , which is why Xiao Jie sprouts later but is taller than Xiao Li. , which is the lesson Xiao Li learns the night Xiao Jie snaps. . The riddle Lao Zhu asks is , and the answer — a human — is correct because of how humans move at different stages of life: crawling, walking, then with a stick.

Tap any underlined claim to see the science behind it.

1

Some bamboo species — like Phyllostachys edulis, the moso bamboo — can put on up to ~91 cm of growth in a single day. The speed comes from cells dividing within already-formed internodes, not from new tissue building up slowly the way trees grow rings.

2

Bamboo is hollow with fibrous bundles running its length. Silica in the cell walls and the hollow culm let it bend low under wind loads and spring back. Solid-trunked trees can't flex the same way, which is why they snap in the storms bamboo survives.

3

The lunar cycle takes about 29.5 days. The moon's appearance moves through new → waxing crescent → first quarter (half) → waxing gibbous → full → waning gibbous → third quarter → waning crescent. The diary's chosen entries follow that order from new through full correctly.

4

From Sophocles's Oedipus Rex, around 5th century BCE. The Sphinx of Thebes posed the riddle to travellers; failure meant death. Oedipus's answer — a human, who crawls as an infant, walks as an adult, and uses a cane in old age — caused the Sphinx to destroy itself.

The day of the first new moon of the year

Dear Diary,

Hello! I am Xiao Li. I live in China and my favourite hobby is talking to the oldest bamboo plant in the grove, Lao Zhu — which translates to Old Stem. He stood tall at the edge of the grove, its stem still thick and strong despite withstanding countless seasons. Everyone looked up to him for his wisdom and wits.

Today, he gave me a riddle and even though I solved all of his other riddles, I can't solve this one. You can help me solve it, can't you? What walks on four legs in the morning, two legs in the afternoon and three legs in the evening? I can't seem to solve it because I don't know which animal has three legs! At least, I think it is an animal because all the animals I've seen have legs! If you have any ideas, maybe you can write back?…somehow.

Your new friend,
Xiao Li

The day of the first half moon of the year

Dear Diary,

Today, I had the most splendiferous day. A group of giants with two legs came and watered me and all the other plants! I wonder why they had come. They also gave water to Xiao Jie, which only sprouted up about a week ago. Despite that, he is much taller than me. Every time I see him, I feel so ashamed because even though bamboo grows very fast, I'm still very much shorter than him. Why is the world so unfair? And I haven't even started on the name calling! He calls me names like Little Weed, Weak Stem, and even says that I am a waste of space, sunlight and water! How infuriating! Well anyway, dear Diary, Xiao Jie aside, today was awesome. Good night!

Your friend,
Xiao Li

The day of the first waxing gibbous of the year

Dear Diary,

I have been trying to grow taller, but nothing much has changed. I went to Lao Zhu for advice, but he just chuckled and told me that taller does not mean better, and better doesn't mean taller. When I asked him what was better, he said, "Even the tallest plant snaps under weight!" Always speaking in riddles, Lao Zhu! I've been thinking, though, maybe my stem is a bit strained, and that Xiao Jie's stem is under a lot of strain too, so maybe it is for the better that I am so short. Tall trees sway so crazily in the wind it scares me.

Your blissfully short friend,
Xiao Li

The day of the first full moon of the year

Dear Diary,

Today was the scariest day of my life. I was photosynthesising as normal when something hit. I can still vividly remember the deafening sound of thunder and the pale colour of the streaks of lightning. The wind seemed to whistle an ominous song in my roots as the raindrops darted down in a relentless torrent. I must've turned as white as a panda's fur. Some of my leaves snapped off as I struggled to stand straight when I heard a soft, gentle voice. "Relax, don't fight it," it said. Deciding I had nothing to lose, I obeyed. As I let the wind rush on me, I slowly bent backwards.

I was one of the lucky ones though. Xiao Jie — he snapped in half. His stem must have been under unbearable strain. The last thing I remember was Xiao Jie fighting the forces of the wind and struggling to stand as tall as he could until his stem just snapped. I am still recovering from the shock. Six other plants suffered the same fate. Their funeral is tomorrow. I know now: bending is better than breaking. Those who fight against our mother — Mother Nature — shall be punished. That is her rule.

Your mercifully lucky friend,
Xiao Li

The day of the fifth full moon of the year

Dear Diary,

It feels like forever since I last talked to you. Lao Zhu is still alive and talking in riddles. The giants are still coming. My only explanation is that there is now a drought and they are taking care of us.

Oh! Also, I am no longer the shortest shoot in the grove. Two days ago, a tiny shoot sprouted and we all call him Xiao Xing Cun, Little Survivor. A few days ago, I was poring over my old entries and found that riddle. Remember? What walks on four legs in the morning, two legs in the afternoon and three legs in the evening? That one. When I showed it to Lao Zhu, he told me the answer: a human! You may be confused, like I was, so I dug a little deeper and realised that those giants that gave us water were humans! Why? Well, at the start of their lives, they crawl on all fours. When they grow up, they learn to walk on two legs, and when they are old, they use something called a walking stick. Well, that is the third leg.

Lao Zhu then gave me something else to think about. He said that life is a circle. And now, looking back on the last — well, I can't remember how long, I realise how true it is. Just as I once looked up to others, now Xiao Xing Cun looks up to me.

Your much taller friend,
Xiao Li

A waxing gibbous is the cycle after the half moon, but before the full moon.

PSLE 2225: Snippets of the Future

Contributor, Singapore Writers' Festival 2025. A short piece imagining what Singapore's most stressful exam looks like two centuries from now.

By 2225, PSLE is gone — replaced by the Planetary Survival Leadership Exam, where 12-year-olds lead teams through simulated crises: asteroid strikes, rising sea levels, or bubble tea shortage.

Morning assembly still happens under the Singapore sun (because tradition), though climate domes keep out the 45°C heat while rotating through four seasons for "training". Students attend the Living Campus, a sentient school where classrooms morph into holographic kampongs, galaxies or labs, while animals roam free as student companions.

Uniforms remain (because heritage!), now self-cleaning and biometric. In the canteen, chicken wings still sell out first, even with mind-preorders. Some things never change.

What's next

The next thing I want to explore more deeply is marine biology, not just by reading books, but by learning how scientists study marine life and ecosystems in the real world. Through future seasons of Singapore Science Stories, I hope to turn what I learn into episodes that make science feel more interesting and accessible to other kids.

I'd like to continue improving my coding and game design skills. After building a simplified version of Climate Crusaders, I realised that educational games can teach people complex ideas in a more engaging way. In the future, I hope to expand the game with more detailed levels, stronger storytelling, and environmental challenges that require players to think about different solutions for different problems.

It would be awesome if I can continue creating projects that help people become curious about science and see everyday issues from a different perspective.
